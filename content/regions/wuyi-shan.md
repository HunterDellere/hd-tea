<span class="section-anchor" id="place"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">The place</span>
  <span class="sh-native">丹霞</span>
  <span class="sh-en">Geography</span>
</div>

<p>Wuyi Shan is in the northwest corner of Fujian, on the Jiangxi border. It is a Danxia landform of red sandstone, weathered into cliffs, gorges, and pillars, with the Nine-Bend River (九曲溪, Jiǔqū Xī) running through it. UNESCO listed the area as a mixed natural and cultural World Heritage site in 1999. The tea gardens are small and scattered because the terrain leaves no room for anything larger; they sit in pockets of weathered rock soil between the cliffs rather than in plantation rows.</p>

<p>The core gardens sit at roughly 200 to 600 m, which is not high. What Wuyi has instead is the soil and the shelter: mineral grit shed by the cliffs, drainage sharp enough to keep the bushes lean, and gorge walls that shade the plants for part of the day.</p>

<span class="section-anchor" id="zhengyan"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">The cliffs and the rank of ground</span>
  <span class="sh-native">正岩</span>
  <span class="sh-en">Terroir</span>
</div>

<p>The Wuyi trade grades ground into ranks, and the price of the leaf follows the rank. <strong>Zhengyan</strong> (正岩, "true cliff") is the protected core inside the scenic reserve. <strong>Banyan</strong> (半岩, "half cliff") is the ground just outside it. River-flat and outside-area leaf sit below both. Inside zhengyan, individual gorges have their own reputations; Rou Gui from Niulankeng (牛栏坑) sells for enough that "Niulankeng Rou Gui" has become a shorthand for the top of the market.</p>

<div class="note" data-mark="岩">
  <span class="note-label">岩韵 yán yùn · rock rhyme</span>
  <p>Yán yùn, "rock rhyme," is the quality the whole grading system is chasing: a mineral depth and a long, cooling finish that zhengyan leaf is said to have and outside leaf is said to lack. Producers do not agree on where it comes from. Some credit the soil, some the roasting, and it is hard to separate the two on the cliffs where both are extreme. Tasting a real Niulankeng next to a well-roasted banyan is the usual way to hear the difference for yourself.</p>
</div>

<span class="section-anchor" id="teas"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">What grows here</span>
  <span class="sh-native">岩茶</span>
  <span class="sh-en">The teas</span>
</div>

<p>Wuyi makes oolong, and its version is the darkest and most heavily roasted of the main oolong traditions. Two cultivars do most of the work. <strong>Shui Xian</strong> (水仙, "water sprite") is the older of the two, and old plantings of it are sold as lao cong, "old bush." <strong>Rou Gui</strong> (肉桂, "cassia") is more recent and now more sought after. The famous name, Da Hong Pao (大红袍), is a special case: the original mother bushes on a ledge at Tianxin are no longer picked, so anything sold as Da Hong Pao today is either propagated from cuttings or, more often, a producer's blend made to the style.</p>

<span class="section-anchor" id="season"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">The year</span>
  <span class="sh-native">焙火</span>
  <span class="sh-en">Harvest and roast</span>
</div>

<p>Picking happens once a year, in late April and May, and takes a mature pluck of three to four leaves rather than the buds a green tea wants. Most of the work comes after that. The leaf is withered, bruised, oxidized to the middle of the range, and fired, then charcoal-roasted in stages across weeks or months with rests in between. A traditional roaster often will not sell the tea until autumn, and some hold it longer than that. There is a practical reason to wait: a fresh roast tastes mostly of char, and the stone-fruit and mineral notes only come forward once the fire has settled, which takes months.</p>
