<span class="section-anchor" id="character"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">Character</span>
  <span class="sh-native">桂皮香</span>
  <span class="sh-en">The cup</span>
</div>

<p>Rou Gui means cassia, the Chinese cinnamon. The cultivar's marker is a sweet bark-spice aroma that survives even a heavy roast, and around it sit the usual Wuyi notes: roasted stone fruit and a mineral finish that carries past the swallow. Next to Shui Xian, the other main Wuyi cultivar, Rou Gui comes across as sharper and more aromatic, where Shui Xian is broader and woodier. Tasting the two together is a common starting point for learning yancha.</p>

<span class="section-anchor" id="origin"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">Origin and rise</span>
  <span class="sh-native">武夷</span>
  <span class="sh-en">Background</span>
</div>

<p>The cultivar is native to Wuyi, but its current status is recent. Most Rou Gui planting dates from the late twentieth century, when it spread through the zhengyan gorges because it was aromatic and took well to charcoal roasting. It now grows on the most expensive ground in Wuyi, and leaf from Niulankeng is sold under the gorge's name. The <a href="../regions/wuyi-shan.html">Wuyi Shan</a> entry explains the ground ranks behind those prices.</p>

<span class="section-anchor" id="session"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">The session</span>
  <span class="sh-native">工夫</span>
  <span class="sh-en">Brewing</span>
</div>

<p>Brew Rou Gui with boiling water and keep the early steeps short. The roast dominates the first two infusions, the cassia note usually shows up around the third, and the tea turns sweeter as the session goes on. A good zhengyan Rou Gui will give nine steeps or more, so there is little reason to stop at three.</p>

<div class="note" data-mark="焙">
  <span class="note-label">Rest the roast</span>
  <p>A freshly roasted Rou Gui bought in summer often tastes of char and little else. That is normal, not a defect; the fire needs a few months to settle before the fruit and mineral notes come through. If a new tin tastes harsh, seal it and try it again in the autumn.</p>
</div>
