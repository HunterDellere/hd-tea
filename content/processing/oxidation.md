<span class="section-anchor" id="reaction"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">The reaction</span>
  <span class="sh-native">酶</span>
  <span class="sh-en">What happens</span>
</div>

<p>All tea is one plant, Camellia sinensis. The six types diverge on a single question: how far the picked leaf is allowed to oxidize before the process is stopped. When a leaf is bruised or torn, enzymes (chiefly polyphenol oxidase) meet the polyphenols inside the cell and, in the presence of oxygen, convert them into larger colored compounds. The leaf browns, the grassy notes recede, and the liquor deepens from pale green toward amber and red. It is the same reaction that browns a cut apple.</p>

<p>Most of what a tea maker does is control this one variable and choose when to stop it. Stopping is done with heat: <strong>kill-green</strong> (杀青, shājīng) denatures the enzymes in a hot pan or steam bath, freezing the leaf wherever it is on the scale. Green tea is killed early, so it stays green. Black tea is oxidized fully first, then dried. Everything else lives in between.</p>

<span class="section-anchor" id="scale"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">The six types on one axis</span>
  <span class="sh-native">六大茶类</span>
  <span class="sh-en">The scale</span>
</div>

<p>Laying the types on an oxidation axis is the clearest map of what the words mean. The figure below is schematic: real oxidation is uneven across a leaf and hard to measure precisely, and the Chinese categories are defined as much by process as by a percentage. Two cases break the simple line and matter most. Yellow tea is lightly oxidized like green but adds a slow damp-stacking step (闷黄, mènhuáng) that rounds off the grassiness. Dark tea, including shou puer, is barely oxidized in the enzymatic sense at all; its color and depth come from microbial fermentation after kill-green, a different reaction that the single axis cannot show.</p>

<span class="section-anchor" id="taste"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">What you taste</span>
  <span class="sh-native">滋味</span>
  <span class="sh-en">In the cup</span>
</div>

<p>Moving up the scale trades one set of qualities for another. Low oxidation keeps fresh, vegetal, and floral notes and a brisker mouthfeel; the liquor stays pale. High oxidation builds malty, fruity, and honeyed notes with a rounder body and a red-amber liquor. Roast, applied afterward, is a separate axis that adds toast, caramel, and char on top of wherever oxidation left the leaf. A heavily roasted, mid-oxidized Wuyi oolong and a lightly roasted one can sit at the same point on this scale and still taste nothing alike, which is why the <a href="../regions/wuyi-shan.html">Wuyi</a> producers argue about fire as much as about ground.</p>
