<span class="section-anchor" id="plant"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">The plant</span>
  <span class="sh-native">菊</span>
  <span class="sh-en">Botany</span>
</div>

<p>Two different plants share the name. <strong>German chamomile</strong> (Matricaria chamomilla, also catalogued as M. recutita) is an annual daisy and the one in almost every commercial blend; <strong>Roman chamomile</strong> (Chamaemelum nobile) is a low perennial with a harsher, more herbaceous cup. The name comes from the Greek chamaimēlon, "earth apple," which is still the best two-word tasting note the flower has. Only the flower heads are picked and dried; stems and leaf are padding, and a tin that shows whole golden heads rather than dust is the quality signal to buy on.</p>

<span class="section-anchor" id="cup"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">The cup</span>
  <span class="sh-native">湯</span>
  <span class="sh-en">Character</span>
</div>

<p>Brewed well, chamomile pours a bright straw gold and tastes of dried apple, honey, and hay, with a light bitterness underneath. Its aroma sits in a volatile oil that escapes with the steam, so the pot or mug should stay covered while it steeps. The flower has a long record of traditional evening use in Europe, and the EMA's herbal monograph documents that traditional-use status. This atlas reports the tradition and leaves it there; what chamomile does for a particular person is a question for their physician, not a tea page.</p>

<!--STEEP_CURVE-->

<div class="note" data-mark="花">
  <span class="note-label">One steep, done well</span>
  <p>Chamomile does not reward the repeated short steeps that leaf teas do; it gives most of what it has in a single infusion. Use boiling water, a full five minutes, and a lid. A short steep leaves you with perfumed water, and the honeyed depth only comes out with the longer stand.</p>
</div>
