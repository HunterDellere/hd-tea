<span class="section-anchor" id="idea"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">The idea</span>
  <span class="sh-native">工夫</span>
  <span class="sh-en">Method</span>
</div>

<p>Gongfu brewing runs a high leaf-to-water ratio, around 5 g to 100 ml, through a small vessel and a series of short steeps, so the same leaves are brewed 5, 8, sometimes 12 times. A Western mug asks one question: what does the cup taste like after 4 minutes? This method asks the tea across a whole session instead. Each infusion is its own cup, and the interesting teas change from steep to steep: a Wuyi oolong that opens on roast, turns to spice, then fades to sweet water tells that story only when you brew it this way. The name 工夫 means skill earned through practice, the same phrase behind gongfu as in martial arts.</p>

<span class="section-anchor" id="parameters"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">The grammar of a session</span>
  <span class="sh-native">参数</span>
  <span class="sh-en">Parameters</span>
</div>

<p>Every steep sheet in this atlas is written in one order: temperature, then leaf-to-water ratio, then vessel, then the times for each infusion. A typical roasted oolong session runs boiling water, around 5 grams of leaf to 100 ml in a gaiwan, a quick rinse to wake the leaf, then steeps that start near five seconds and lengthen as the leaf gives out. The ratio is the part that surprises people coming from mug brewing: it is roughly four times as much leaf per volume, which is what makes the short steeps work.</p>

<div class="meta-list">
  <div><dt>Water</dt><dd>100°C for roasted oolong, puer, black</dd></div>
  <div><dt>Ratio</dt><dd><span class="mono">5 g : 100 ml</span> (adjust to the vessel)</dd></div>
  <div><dt>Vessel</dt><dd>gaiwan or small clay pot</dd></div>
  <div><dt>Rinse</dt><dd>one fast pour, discarded, for rolled or aged leaf</dd></div>
  <div><dt>Steeps</dt><dd>start short, lengthen as flavor drops</dd></div>
</div>

<p>Lower the temperature for delicate leaf: greens and light oolongs want 80 to 90°C, and the same short-steep logic applies with a gentler hand. The <a href="../teas/rou-gui.html">Rou Gui</a> entry shows a full nine-steep curve for a roasted Wuyi oolong; read the curve as a shape, not a recipe, since your leaf, water, and vessel will move the numbers.</p>

<div class="note" data-mark="盖">
  <span class="note-label">Why the gaiwan</span>
  <p>The lidded bowl gives you close control of the pour and lets you smell the wet leaf between steeps, which tells you a lot about how the session is going. A clay pot holds heat better and suits heavy roasts and puer, but it absorbs the character of whatever it brews, so it is worth keeping a pot to a single type.</p>
</div>
