<span class="section-anchor" id="elevation"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">Elevation and cloud</span>
  <span class="sh-native">高山</span>
  <span class="sh-en">The mountain</span>
</div>

<p>In Taiwan the trade draws its "high mountain" line at roughly 1,000 m, and the mechanism behind that number is real. Higher gardens are colder, so the bush grows more slowly and the new leaf stays tender longer. They also spend more of the day inside cloud, and shade changes the leaf's chemistry: with less direct sun, the plant accumulates more amino acids (theanine above all) relative to the bitter catechins, which reads in the cup as sweetness, thickness, and less astringency. The best-known gardens on Alishan and Lishan sit well above that line.</p>

<p>Altitude on its own does not do this. What matters is the combination that comes with it: the cold, the cloud, and the wide swing between day and night temperature. A garden at 800 m on a north-facing, fog-pooling slope can beat one at 1,200 m in full sun, so aspect and cloud pattern do as much work as the elevation figure.</p>

<div class="note" data-mark="雾">
  <span class="note-label">Why shade sweetens</span>
  <p>Japanese growers reproduce this at sea level on purpose. Gyokuro and tencha are grown under covers for the last few weeks before picking, cutting the leaf's light to raise amino acids and lower bitterness. The cover does by hand roughly what the mountain cloud does on its own.</p>
</div>

<span class="section-anchor" id="soil"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">Soil and slope</span>
  <span class="sh-native">土壤</span>
  <span class="sh-en">The ground</span>
</div>

<p>Tea wants acidic, free-draining soil and does badly with wet feet, which is why so many famous gardens sit on slopes. Beyond drainage, the mineral content of the ground is the part producers most often tie to flavor. The clearest case is <a href="../regions/wuyi-shan.html">Wuyi</a>, where the weathered rock grit of the cliffs is credited with the mineral "rock rhyme" of yancha, and where the trade prices the same cultivar differently depending on which gorge it grew in. How much of that is soil chemistry and how much is the microclimate of a sheltered gorge is genuinely unsettled; both are real, and they arrive together.</p>

<span class="section-anchor" id="cultivar"></span>
<div class="section-head">
  <span class="sh-title">Cultivar meets place</span>
  <span class="sh-native">品种</span>
  <span class="sh-en">The variety</span>
</div>

<p>Terroir always acts on a particular cultivar, and you cannot hold one constant while talking about the other. A cultivar bred for a place, like Taiwan's Jin Xuan (TTES No. 12, released in 1981), brings its own baseline of aroma and body; grow it somewhere else and you get a version that is recognizable but shifted. A careful garden records both the variety and the ground for that reason. The region pages in this atlas name each region's signature cultivars, and the tea pages name the cultivar behind the cup.</p>
